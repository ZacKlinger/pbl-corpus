# Claim-evidence: assessment and rigor in PBL

This thread catalogs how the field documents the assessment-of-PBL question and how rigor is preserved when the unit of work is a project. Empirical-layer entries describe what has been measured about assessment-in-PBL; practitioner-layer entries describe how leading frameworks specify assessment as part of project design.

---

## Claim: Standardized achievement tests are documented as inadequate for measuring the outcomes PBL aims to produce, creating a structural assessment mismatch that complicates evaluation.

### Supporting evidence

- "Evaluations of its effectiveness have been hampered by the paucity of valid, reliable, and readily usable measures of the kinds of deeper learning and interpersonal and intrapersonal competencies that PBL aims to promote." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. iii — layer: empirical
- "Often, assessments do not measure the array of cognitive and noncognitive (intra- and interpersonal) outcomes that deeper learning approaches intend to produce" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 10, citing NRC 2012 — layer: empirical

### Open questions

- The Pellegrino & Hilton (NRC 2012) report on deeper-learning measurement is the canonical anchor for this claim. Worth reading in primary text.

## Claim: Rigor and PBL implementation correlate but do not entail each other — PBL can be implemented with low rigor, and rigor can be present without PBL.

### Supporting evidence

- "It found that higher levels of PBL implementation were associated with higher perceptions of rigor, but also that academic rigor can be present without PBL and that PBL can be implemented with low levels of rigor." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 29, summarizing Edmunds et al. (2017) — layer: empirical

### Open questions

- The Edmunds et al. (2017) study uses a "perceptions of rigor" construct. Worth reading in primary text to assess whether perceived rigor is being treated as a proxy for measured rigor.

## Claim: HQPBL's Public Product criterion makes public exhibition and critique an assessment commitment, not just a culminating event.

### Supporting evidence

- "In a high quality project, students make their work public by sharing it not only with the teacher but also with each other, experts, and other people beyond the classroom. This public process and final presentation encourages students to improve the quality of their work and demonstrates what students know and can do." — [HQPBL 2018](../sources/HQPBL-2018-framework.md) — p. 3 — layer: practitioner
- "Public Product: Students' work is publicly displayed, discussed, and critiqued." — [HQPBL 2018](../sources/HQPBL-2018-framework.md) — p. 3 — layer: practitioner

### Open questions

- EL Education's "Models of Excellence" archive and High Tech High's defense-of-learning practices are the most-documented practitioner accounts of how public exhibition functions as assessment. Read in primary text to populate this claim with the specific assessment practices the practitioner layer documents.
- Wiggins & McTighe's *Understanding by Design* (1998) is cited across the practitioner layer as the backward-design architecture under PBL planning. Worth reading for assessment-design thread.

## Claim: Performance assessments and student-produced artifacts are the assessment forms the literature names as fitting PBL — but their reliability and scoring validity remain undeveloped.

### Supporting evidence

- "Performance-based assessments involve students' completing tasks that demonstrate their capacity to apply new knowledge and skills; they also require that students respond to a question or performance task versus selecting a correct answer, as they would on a non-analytical multiple-choice test" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 30–31, citing Darling-Hammond and Adamson (2010) — layer: empirical
- "Student artifacts, 'external representations' of students' 'constructed knowledge,' are often cited in the PBL literature as an appropriate assessment for a PBL environment" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 31, citing Grant and Branch (2005); Krajcik and Shin (2014) — layer: empirical
- "While the use of artifacts certainly appears to be a promising strategy, teachers may find it difficult to score artifacts in a valid and reliable way" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 31, citing Aslan and Reigeluth (2015) — layer: empirical

### Open questions

- The PBLWorks rubrics (Critical Thinking, Collaboration, Creativity & Innovation, Presentation, Self-management) are the practitioner layer's most-developed response to the scoring-validity problem. Read in primary text.

## Claim: HQPBL's Reflection criterion treats reflection as embedded across the project, not as an end-of-project capstone.

### Supporting evidence

- "Reflection: Students reflect on their work and their learning throughout the project." — [HQPBL 2018](../sources/HQPBL-2018-framework.md) — p. 4 — layer: practitioner
- "They pause regularly—not just at the end of the project, but throughout the process—to think about what they are doing and learning." — [HQPBL 2018](../sources/HQPBL-2018-framework.md) — p. 4 — layer: practitioner

### Open questions

- How does this differ from EL Education's protocols for critique and revision, or High Tech High's presentations of learning? Each practitioner framework specifies reflection somewhat differently. Synthesis pending more primary reading.

# Claim-evidence: what the PBL literature has measured

This thread catalogs the descriptive state of the PBL empirical evidence base — outcomes studied, populations studied, methodological limits, and where the field has converged or diverged. It is descriptive (what the literature says about itself), not adjudicative.

---

## Claim: The current state of evidence for PBL effectiveness is best characterized as "promising but not proven."

### Supporting evidence

- "The working paper suggests that the evidence for PBL's effectiveness in improving students' outcomes is 'promising but not proven.'" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. iii (Abstract) — layer: empirical
- "most of the quantitative outcome studies reviewed in this paper must be considered descriptive because concerns about internal validity preclude causal inferences." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 53 — layer: empirical

### Open questions

- The Saavedra (2021) AP Government and AP Environmental Science RCTs and the Krajcik (2023) Multiple Literacies in PBL third-grade science RCT post-date Condliffe and are designed specifically to address the "few rigorous RCTs" gap that Condliffe identified. Read those in primary text to update this claim.
- The Chen & Yang (2019) meta-analysis is the most-cited recent quantitative synthesis. It is paywalled (Elsevier); currently a lead.

## Claim: PBL outcome evidence is unevenly distributed across subjects — science and social studies have substantially more empirical support than math or English/language arts.

### Supporting evidence

- "some studies have found positive effects associated with the use of PBL curricula in science and social studies classes. Evidence for its effectiveness in math and literacy classes is more limited. In particular, it has been noted that math teachers have found it difficult to integrate PBL into their instruction." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. iii (Abstract) — layer: empirical
- "Many more studies on the PBL and student learning relationship are being conducted in science and social studies classrooms than in math and English/language arts classrooms. It is difficult to know whether this imbalance is related to a PBL approach being better suited for science and social studies classrooms or, rather, reflects the fact that many of the PBL social studies and science curricula were developed by, or in partnership with, university researchers." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 49 — layer: empirical

### Open questions

- Is the math gap evidence of unsuitability or evidence of a sparse research base on math PBL? Condliffe does not adjudicate this; the field has not produced a clear answer.

## Claim: The lack of valid, reliable measures of intra- and interpersonal competencies has been a structural constraint on PBL outcome research.

### Supporting evidence

- "Evaluations of its effectiveness have been hampered by the paucity of valid, reliable, and readily usable measures of the kinds of deeper learning and interpersonal and intrapersonal competencies that PBL aims to promote." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. iii (Abstract) — layer: empirical
- "Studies investigating the relationship between PBL and cognitive competencies continue to far outnumber investigations of the relationship between PBL and intra- and interpersonal competencies. This difference is likely related to the greater availability of valid and reliable measures of certain cognitive skills" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 54 — layer: empirical

### Open questions

- The Pellegrino & Hilton (NRC 2012) deeper-learning framework is the cognitive/intrapersonal/interpersonal taxonomy Condliffe uses. Worth reading in primary text for thread #5 anchoring.

## Claim: Deeper Learning Network schools (where PBL is the most-common instructional approach but is bundled with other reform elements) show better outcomes than comparison schools — but the contribution of PBL specifically cannot be isolated.

### Supporting evidence

- "Network school students also achieved higher scores on state-mandated English language arts and math tests" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 48, citing Zeiser et al. (2014) — layer: empirical
- "Students in the Network schools were about 8 percentage points more likely to graduate from high school on time (that is, within four years after entering ninth grade) than were students from comparison schools — on-time graduation rates were 65.4 percent and 57.5 percent, respectively" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 48, citing Zeiser, Mills, Wallach, and Garet (2016) — layer: empirical

### Counter-evidence (limits of the finding)

- "the model and intensity of PBL varied across the schools in the study and ... PBL was just one of many features of the Network schools that could have driven these effects." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 48 — layer: empirical
- "although the researchers controlled statistically for differences in observed background characteristics, students were not randomly assigned to Deeper Learning schools. As a result, the better outcomes they experienced may be related to unobserved differences" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 48 — layer: empirical

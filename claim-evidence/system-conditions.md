# Claim-evidence: system conditions PBL needs to function

This thread catalogs what the implementation literature documents as the system-level conditions under which PBL reaches high fidelity. The literature consistently locates the constraints on PBL implementation in system conditions — PD hours, planning time, schedule structure, leadership support, curriculum integration — not in individual teacher capability. The corpus preserves that framing.

---

## Claim: PBL implementation with fidelity requires sustained professional development — both initial training and ongoing support during implementation.

### Supporting evidence

- "PBL implementation research has strongly suggested that it will be difficult for any PBL model to be implemented with fidelity if it does not include professional development." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 33 — layer: empirical
- "Given these challenges, professional development — both initial training and continuing support — is likely to be essential to the successful implementation of PBL." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. iii (Abstract) — layer: empirical
- "Students at the school where fidelity of PBL implementation was highest registered the greatest gains over time in achievement on state math and science tests. On the other hand, performance of students at the school with the lowest fidelity of implementation remained essentially unchanged." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 33, footnote 26, summarizing Capraro et al. (2016) — layer: empirical

### Open questions

- Specific modes of PD (online supports, on-site coaching, in-person trainings, educative curriculum materials, conferences) have not been rigorously compared. Condliffe calls this out as a top recommendation for future research (p. 52). Read the Lucas Education Research RCT designs to see how this gap has been addressed since 2017.

## Claim: School leadership and school culture alignment are moderators of PBL implementation fidelity.

### Supporting evidence

- "Overall, PBL implementation was the strongest in reform model schools, where PBL was often seen as a core instructional component, encouraged or required by school leadership, and viewed as consistent with the school's student-centered culture." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 32, on Ravitz (2010) — layer: empirical
- "the research on instructional practices used in schools participating in the Deeper Learning Network also suggested that PBL implementation is easier when it is encouraged by school leadership and aligned with school culture" — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 32 — layer: empirical
- "School and School System Leaders: I commit to building the capacity and creating the conditions necessary for educators to implement High Quality Project Based Learning as described by the six HQPBL criteria." — [HQPBL 2018](../sources/HQPBL-2018-framework.md) — p. 5 (commitment statement) — layer: practitioner

### Open questions

- Condliffe (p. 51) notes that "aside from making clear that supportive school leadership facilitates implementation, the research has not yet clarified how PBL implementation is affected by the broader school context." The mechanism by which leadership support produces fidelity (resource allocation? schedule protection? signal of legitimacy?) is undertheorized.

---

## Claim: District-level conditions — pacing guides, standardized-testing pressure, teacher mobility — measurably interfere with PBL implementation.

### Supporting evidence

- (Condliffe summarizing Marx et al. 2004 on LeTUS in Detroit:) "Marx and colleagues cited a number of district contextual factors that influenced the implementation of their PBL instructional reform strategy for science: teacher mobility, difficulty with technology maintenance, competing demands of standardized testing, and staff changes at the district level. In another study (Dole, Bloom, and Kowalske, 2016), district mandates and pacing guides were also found to interfere with PBL implementation." — [Condliffe 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — p. 31 — layer: empirical

### Open questions

- Most of this evidence is from single-district case studies. The empirical literature has not produced a multi-district comparative account of how district policy shapes PBL fidelity.

---

## Claim: Structural conditions outside the practitioner frameworks themselves — time for teachers to learn and plan, flexible policies for curriculum delivery and assessment — are explicitly named by the frameworks as necessary for the criteria to be implementable.

### Supporting evidence

- "Other factors such as classroom and school culture are important for the effectiveness of PBL, as are structural conditions such as adequate time for teachers to learn and plan, and flexible policies for curriculum delivery and assessment." — [HQPBL 2018](../sources/HQPBL-2018-framework.md) — p. 1 — layer: practitioner

### Open questions

- The practitioner frameworks name these structural conditions but do not specify them (how much planning time? what schedule structures?). The implementation literature documents their importance but does not specify thresholds either. This is a gap the corpus should track as more sources are read.

---

## Claim: The implementation literature locates PBL's implementation constraints in system conditions, not in teacher capability.

### Supporting evidence

The Condliffe review documents implementation challenges that, in their *findings*, repeatedly resolve to system-level moderators:

- PD presence/absence is the strongest reported predictor of fidelity (p. 33, p. iii — already quoted above).
- School leadership and school culture moderate implementation (p. 32 — quoted above).
- District pacing guides and standardized-testing pressure interfere with implementation (p. 31 — quoted above).
- HQPBL itself names "adequate time for teachers to learn and plan, and flexible policies for curriculum delivery and assessment" as structural conditions outside the criteria (HQPBL 2018, p. 1).

### Counter-evidence / framing nuance

- Condliffe's *framing* language sometimes locates difficulty in teacher beliefs and practice: e.g., teachers must "modify their roles (from directors to facilitators of learning) and ... tolerate not only ambiguity but also more noise and movement in the classroom" (p. iii). The corpus reads this as a description of the practice shift PBL demands, not as a story about teacher capability — because Condliffe's own findings consistently locate the *moderator* of whether that shift succeeds in PD, leadership, and school context. The system-condition framing is what the literature actually documents.

### Open questions

- The Mehta & Fine (2019) *In Search of Deeper Learning* practitioner-empirical record reports where deeper learning was and was not happening inside U.S. high schools. Worth reading to test whether their account also locates the moderators in system conditions or extends the picture in some way.

## Thesis

The HQPBL Framework is a six-page practitioner consensus document, facilitated by the Buck Institute for Education, that proposes a *student-experience-side* description of high-quality PBL — explicitly distinguishing itself from PBL design frameworks written from the teacher's perspective. The framework names six criteria — Intellectual Challenge and Accomplishment, Authenticity, Public Product, Collaboration, Project Management, and Reflection — and frames each as a set of guiding questions about what students experience. Its argument for existing is that PBL has gained adoption faster than it has gained shared meaning, and a baseline shared description, written from the student side, can give educators a common floor without locking in any single curricular model. The framework is explicit that its six criteria are necessary, not sufficient: school and classroom culture, plus structural conditions like "adequate time for teachers to learn and plan, and flexible policies for curriculum delivery and assessment," are named as additional requirements outside the framework's scope. The document closes with commitment statements scoped to five roles — teachers, school/system leaders, teacher educators, policy leaders and journalists, and curriculum and support providers — which is itself an argument that the conditions for PBL are distributed across the system, not located in any single role.

- "At the present time, however, there is a lack of agreement about what goes into high quality Project Based Learning." — p. 1 — layer: practitioner
- "The Framework for High Quality Project Based Learning describes PBL in terms of the student experience and is intended to provide educators everywhere with a shared basis for designing and implementing good projects." — p. 1 — layer: practitioner
- "It describes six criteria, each of which must be at least minimally present in a project in order for it to be judged 'high quality.'" — p. 1 — layer: practitioner
- "The presence of a criterion, however, is only a beginning. Each criterion can be judged in turn as to the quality of its implementation." — p. 1 — layer: practitioner
- "Other factors such as classroom and school culture are important for the effectiveness of PBL, as are structural conditions such as adequate time for teachers to learn and plan, and flexible policies for curriculum delivery and assessment." — p. 1 — layer: practitioner
- "Intellectual Challenge and Accomplishment: Students learn deeply, think critically, and strive for excellence." — p. 2 (criterion 1 headline) — layer: practitioner
- "Projects should not just be 'fun activities' or 'hands-on experiences' requiring minimal intellectual effort. A high quality project requires students to think critically about a complex problem, question, or issue with multiple answers, and then work on that project over the course of days, weeks, and even months." — p. 2 — layer: practitioner
- "Authenticity: Students work on projects that are meaningful and relevant to their culture, their lives, and their future." — p. 2 (criterion 2 headline) — layer: practitioner
- "A high quality project reflects what happens in the world outside of school. It uses the tools, techniques, and technology found there." — p. 2 — layer: practitioner
- "Public Product: Students' work is publicly displayed, discussed, and critiqued." — p. 3 (criterion 3 headline) — layer: practitioner
- "In a high quality project, students make their work public by sharing it not only with the teacher but also with each other, experts, and other people beyond the classroom." — p. 3 — layer: practitioner
- "Collaboration: Students collaborate with other students in person or online and/or receive guidance from adult mentors and experts." — p. 3 (criterion 4 headline) — layer: practitioner
- "When students truly collaborate, they are contributing individual voices, talents, and skills to a shared piece of work, while respecting the contributions of others." — p. 3 — layer: practitioner
- "Project Management: Students use a project management process that enables them to proceed effectively from project initiation to completion." — p. 4 (criterion 5 headline) — layer: practitioner
- "In high quality PBL, students learn and make use of project management processes, tools, and strategies similar to those used in the world beyond school. Students may also follow the steps of design thinking as they manage projects." — p. 4 — layer: practitioner
- "Reflection: Students reflect on their work and their learning throughout the project." — p. 4 (criterion 6 headline) — layer: practitioner
- "They pause regularly—not just at the end of the project, but throughout the process—to think about what they are doing and learning." — p. 4 — layer: practitioner
- "School and School System Leaders: I commit to building the capacity and creating the conditions necessary for educators to implement High Quality Project Based Learning as described by the six HQPBL criteria." — p. 5 (commitment statement) — layer: practitioner

## Where this sits in the canon

- Practitioner-layer anchor for thread #1 (design elements). Distinct from the PBLWorks Gold Standard (Larmer & Mergendoller 2015) seven-element list — same Buck Institute facilitation lineage but with a different unit of description (student experience vs. teacher design moves). Read both together to render the *operative* shared definition of PBL across the practitioner-layer frameworks; defer cross-framework synthesis until at least one EL Education and one New Tech/High Tech High primary source is also read.
- Provides practitioner-layer anchor for thread #3 (assessment and rigor) via the Public Product, Project Management, and Reflection criteria, each of which carries an assessment commitment.
- Provides practitioner-layer counterpart to Condliffe (2017)'s empirical observation that "the model of PBL often drastically differs from study to study" — this framework is one of the field's responses to that fragmentation, written explicitly to give the practitioner community a shared baseline.

## Tensions / contradictions

- The framework's six criteria do not map one-to-one onto the seven Gold Standard PBL essential elements (Larmer & Mergendoller 2015). Both come out of the Buck Institute lineage; HQPBL frames itself in the student experience, Gold Standard in teacher design. The corpus should record both as practitioner-layer descriptions of the same family, not as a single consensus, until the cross-framework mapping is read in primary text.
- The framework's framing of structural conditions is explicit but non-prescriptive: "adequate time for teachers to learn and plan, and flexible policies for curriculum delivery and assessment" are named as conditions outside the six criteria. This is consistent with the implementation-research finding (Condliffe 2017, p. 32–33) that school leadership, PD, and culture are the moderators of fidelity; the practitioner framework concedes the system-condition dependency without specifying it.
- "Need to know" is not a criterion here, consistent with Larmer & Mergendoller's 2015 decision to remove "need to know" from the Gold Standard list (per Condliffe 2017, p. 8 fn. 12). The HQPBL framework does name student voice/choice and authenticity as components of the Authenticity criterion, which is closer to the mechanisms BIE argued produce a "need to know" rather than the construct itself.

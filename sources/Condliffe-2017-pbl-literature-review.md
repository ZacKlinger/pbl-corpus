## Thesis

MDRC's working-paper update of Thomas (2000) surveys the PBL literature published 2000–2017 and reaches a stable, deliberately modest conclusion: the field still lacks consensus on what PBL is, the implementation literature documents predictable challenges, and the outcomes evidence is "promising but not proven." The paper is descriptive — it synthesizes the published design principles (Larmer & Mergendoller; Krajcik & Shin; Parker et al.; Darling-Hammond et al.; Ravitz; Thomas; Grant), maps the three ways PBL enters K-12 settings (externally developed curricula, teacher-initiated PBL, whole-school approaches), inventories the implementation findings (teacher belief shifts, PD needs, classroom-management demands, school-context dependencies, assessment-rigor tensions), and catalogs the outcome studies (mostly science and social studies; very thin in math and ELA; few RCTs; subgroup evidence underdeveloped). Its through-line is that the same lack of a shared, measurable model that frustrates practitioners also prevents the research from producing the kind of evidentiary base that would let the field generalize. The recommendations target both: measurable design principles, more rigorous evaluation methodologies, better intra/interpersonal measures, and serious attention to professional-development and school-context moderators.

## Methods

This is a narrative literature review (a "working paper"), not a meta-analysis. Phase 1 (commissioned by Lucas Education Research, published May 2016) surveyed PBL studies 2000–early 2015, prioritizing peer-reviewed journals, published books, and reports from government agencies, research firms, and educational nonprofits. Phase 2 (the 2017 update, supported by Kresge) added studies published 2015–2017 with a narrower scope focused on implementation and effectiveness. Outcome studies were included only when they had a comparison or control group. "Close cousins" of PBL (problem-based, inquiry-based) were largely excluded, except where problem-based learning units "implicitly or explicitly aligned with the PBL design principles" reviewed (p. 3 fn. 4). The review is descriptive of the field's findings — it does not produce new effect-size estimates.

## Where this sits in the canon

- Updates Thomas (2000) — the prior canonical PBL synthesis. Treat Condliffe 2017 as the current most-cited PBL literature review on the empirical side, anchoring thread #5 ("What the literature describes about PBL").
- Provides cross-references to all the major recent design-principle articulations the practitioner layer turns into frameworks: Larmer & Mergendoller (PBLWorks Gold Standard), Krajcik & Shin (project-based science), Parker et al. (Knowledge in Action), Darling-Hammond et al. (inquiry-based design principles), Ravitz, Grant.
- Provides the empirical grounding for thread #6 ("system conditions PBL needs") via its school-context, leadership, PD, and culture findings.
- Cross-corpus: this paper notes the existence of the Kirschner/Sweller/Clark critique in passing (p. 2). KSC is held in `learning-thesis/sources/`; do not adjudicate that debate here.

## Tensions / contradictions

- Two readings of the lack-of-consensus finding coexist in the paper itself. One argues that "the lack of consensus on what PBL entails presents clear drawbacks for practitioners and researchers" (p. 12). The other concedes that "some might argue that PBL can thrive as a dynamic concept without a concrete definition" and that overly prescriptive design principles can "hold back innovation ... and unintentionally elicit teacher resistance" (p. 12). The paper does not resolve this; it surfaces it.
- The paper documents implementation challenges in language that frequently locates the difficulty in teacher beliefs and practice, but its actual findings repeatedly locate the *moderator* of those challenges in system conditions: sustained PD, supportive school leadership, school culture alignment, schedule structure, district pacing guides, and curriculum-integration support. The corpus should preserve the system-condition framing the findings document, not the teacher-capability framing that appears in some of the paper's framing language.
- BIE/Larmer & Mergendoller removed "need to know" from the Gold Standard design principles list in 2015, calling it "one of the most powerful arguments for PBL" but "not as a thing teachers design" — explicitly classifying it as motivation rather than design principle (p. 8, fn. 12). This is an internal disagreement within the practitioner layer about what counts as a design element vs. an outcome of design.

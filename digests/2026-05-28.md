## What I read this week

- [Condliffe et al. 2017](../sources/Condliffe-2017-pbl-literature-review.md) — MDRC's 84-page synthesis updating Thomas (2000); the most-cited recent empirical PBL literature review. Its load-bearing finding is that PBL effectiveness is "promising but not proven," and that the same lack of a shared, measurable model that frustrates practitioners is what keeps the research from producing generalizable evidence.
- [HQPBL 2018](../sources/HQPBL-2018-framework.md) — the six-page coalition framework facilitated by the Buck Institute, written from the student-experience side. Six criteria: Intellectual Challenge and Accomplishment, Authenticity, Public Product, Collaboration, Project Management, Reflection. It is explicit that structural conditions (planning time, flexible policies) are necessary but outside the framework's scope — which is itself a finding about how the practitioner field thinks about system conditions.

## Tensions I noticed

The most interesting one is internal to Condliffe itself, and I think it's the kind of finding the corpus exists to record:

Condliffe's *framing* language often locates PBL's implementation difficulty in teacher belief and practice shifts ("teachers modify their roles," "tolerate ambiguity," "change their pedagogical beliefs"). But Condliffe's *findings* — the things the empirical studies actually documented — consistently resolve to system-level moderators: presence vs. absence of sustained PD; supportive vs. unsupportive school leadership; cultural alignment with the reform; district pacing guides and standardized-testing pressure; technology access. The teacher-belief paragraphs are descriptions of the shift PBL demands; the moderator-of-fidelity findings are about whether the *system* makes that shift possible.

I preserved both — quoted the teacher-belief language in the source note, but in the `system-conditions.md` thread I drew the claim from the moderator findings rather than the framing language. The footnote in `system-conditions.md` is explicit about this choice. Worth your read — this is exactly the place where the "locate constraints in system conditions" CLAUDE.md instruction has practical consequences for how the corpus represents what Condliffe actually found.

A second tension worth recording: the practitioner field is not internally consistent about whether "need to know" is a design element or an outcome of design. BIE removed it from the Gold Standard list in 2015. New Tech Network still uses NTK as a central instructional move. HQPBL doesn't name it. I recorded this as a claim under design-elements and surfaced the BIE/NTN disagreement explicitly.

## Where pbl-product might draw on this (optional)

Three specific implications surfaced this week, worth flagging but not pushing as decisions:

- The Condliffe finding that **"PBL can be implemented with low levels of rigor"** (Edmunds et al. 2017) is directly relevant to whatever quality-of-implementation signals `pbl-product` plans to surface in teacher/coach views. The product will need to distinguish "this is a project" from "this is a rigorous project," and the empirical literature has documented that the two can come apart.
- The HQPBL framework's choice to **write from the student-experience side** is a useful design lens — if `pbl-product` builds tutoring or coaching features, that student-experience descriptor language (the "to what extent do students…" framing) is closer to what an AI tutor would actually use than the teacher-design-move language of the Gold Standard.
- The **"need to know" disagreement between BIE and New Tech Network** matters if `pbl-product` ever ships project-launch templates — different practitioner traditions have made different choices about whether to design "need-to-know" as an explicit project move or to let it emerge from the design elements that produce it. Don't bake one camp's framing in.
